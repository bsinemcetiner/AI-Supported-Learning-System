Analog vs Digital - Definitions 
An analog audio system sound generates and manipulates continuous electrical signals, mirroring 
the original sound wave. The signal varies smoothly over time (depending on any sound effect), 
keeping sound within the electrical medium.
• Examples: Vinyl, cassette, radio waves, analog mixing consoles, electric guitar amps & pedals
A digital audio system represents sound by converting it into discrete numerical samples (or by 
generating them). The waveform is represented as a sequence of numbers, allowing precise storage, 
editing, and duplication.
• Examples: CDs, any computer representation of sound, audio formats (WAV, MP3), streaming services, 
computer music production tools
Analog vs Digital

The Sampling Theorem
“A signal can be exactly reproduced if it is sampled at a frequency 𝑭, where 𝑭 is twice the 
maximum frequency in the signal.”

Audio signals containing frequencies between 0 and 𝑆 can be exactly represented if 
sampled at a rate of at least 2𝑆 samples per second.

The Sampling Theorem - Aliasing
• On the analog side, aliasing can occur due to poor analog-to-digital converter circuit 
design.
• On the digital side, aliasing can happen due to changing the sampling rate, or using 
effects that introduce new harmonics.
• Overcoming aliasing during analog-digital conversion:
• Input signal goes through a low-pass filter
• Increase sampling rate to easily eliminate high frequencies
• E.g. increase from 44.1 𝑘𝐻𝑧 to 48 𝑘𝐻𝑧, or to 96/192 𝑘𝐻𝑧 in movie/sound 
production settings

Quantization
• Number systems
• One’s complement
• Invert every bit to get negation
• Negative numbers are in reverse order
• If there’s a carry out of the most significant bit, add it back to the least significant bit
• Two’s complement
• Invert every bit and add 1 to get negation
• Ignore overflows
• Overflows naturally result in “wraparound” from positive to negative (and vice-versa).

Quantization - Dither
• Quantization error has interesting characteristics:
• When signal level (amplitude) is high, quantization errors are random.
• Sounds like white noise, perceptually benign
• When it’s low, we don’t use all the quantization levels, leading to an increased 
percentage of errors.
• Errors become more complex, yielding harmonic and intermodulation distortion, 
as well as signal-dependent modulation of the noise floor.
• Dither is small random noise
• Added prior to quantization, randomizing the signal slightly
• Quantization error is no longer a function of the signal
• Turns into white noise
