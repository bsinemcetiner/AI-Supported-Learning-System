Fundamental Concepts of Sound
Sound Travel
• A sound source disturbs the surrounding particles 
in the air (medium).
• The area surrounding the sound source are 
compressed and rarefied.
• Air molecules undergo successive displacements 
that correspond to the original disturbance.
• They arrive at a receptor (e.g. an ear or a 
microphone diaphragm), which moves similarly 
based on the pressure.

Frequency
• The vibrations can occur periodically or aperiodically. (e.g. Violin 
vs cymbal)
• One sequence of a periodic vibration is one cycle (or period)
• Frequency: Number of cycles each second. Measured in Hertz 
(𝐻𝑧), defined as the reciprocal of period.
• Barometric pressure (~10−5 𝐻𝑧)
• Cosmic rays(~1022 𝐻𝑧)
• Sound – roughly the range of human hearing (20 𝐻𝑧 −
20𝑘𝐻𝑧)
Certain frequencies are standardized as concert pitches (e.g. 
𝑐𝑜𝑛𝑐𝑒𝑟𝑡 𝑝𝑖𝑡𝑐ℎ 𝐴 = 440 𝐻𝑧)
Exists on a logarithmic scale. Exhibits interesting mathematical 
relationships, e.g. A5 = 880 𝐻𝑧
Sound Properties
• Wavelength: The distance sound travels through one period.
• E.g. 20 𝑘𝐻𝑧 wavelength is ~1.8cm, 20 𝐻𝑧 wavelength is ~1.7m
• Can’t be received linearly
• Diffraction: Sound bending through openings or 
around obstacles
• Longer wavelengths diffract better
• Refraction: Sound bending because of velocity 
changes
• At night, you can hear better outside. Music also seems 
louder.

Sound Properties (cont)
Things that affect sound:
• Temperature
• Speed of sound (in air): ~1191𝑘𝑚/ℎ at 0 °𝐶, 
~1239 𝑘𝑚/ℎ at 20 °𝐶
• Why woodwind players must warm their instrument before playing
• Elasticity
• Speed of sound through steel is 14 times greater than the speed of sound through air
• Density
• Dense mediums cause the speed of sound to decrease
• Attenuation: Absorption of sound as it travels
• High frequencies are absorbed more prominently
• Why a nearby lightning is heard as a sharp clap, while a faraway one is a low rumble
• Humidity increases attenuation since moist air is less dense

Periodic signals
• Simplest periodic signal, the sine wave
• Operates at a fundamental frequency (𝐹0)
• Complex periodic signals -> series of extra 
frequencies at multiples of 𝐹0 -> Harmonics
• Ex: If 𝐹0 is 220, harmonics will be at 440, 
660, 880, 1100 etc.
• E.g. musical instruments
• The sound color (timbre) is determined by 
harmonics
• Sometimes also called overtones
